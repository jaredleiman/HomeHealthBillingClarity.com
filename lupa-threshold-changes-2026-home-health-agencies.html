LUPA Threshold Changes 2026: How Home Health Agencies Can Avoid Costly Payment Reductions

Published April 2026  ·  HomeHealthBillingClarity.com

CMS recalibrated LUPA thresholds across HHRG categories for CY 2026. For independent home health agencies whose visit patterns fall near any LUPA threshold, this recalibration could mean significantly lower reimbursement on episodes that previously paid at the full rate. Here's what LUPA is, what changed, and how to find out if your agency is exposed.

📊 Illustration: Bar chart of agency visit patterns vs LUPA thresholds by HHRG category — sage green and gold editorial style

What Is a LUPA?

LUPA stands for Low Utilization Payment Adjustment. It's a Medicare payment mechanism that applies when a home health agency delivers fewer visits than a minimum threshold during a 30-day payment period.

Under normal circumstances, Medicare pays home health agencies a fixed episode rate based on the patient's clinical complexity and functional status — their Home Health Resource Group (HHRG) classification. This rate is paid regardless of the exact number of visits delivered, within a reasonable range.

But when visit counts fall below the LUPA threshold for a given HHRG category, Medicare switches from the fixed episode rate to a per-visit payment. Per-visit payments are almost always significantly lower than the corresponding episode rate — often by thousands of dollars per episode.

Why LUPA Thresholds Matter So Much

A single LUPA episode on a patient who should have received full episode payment can represent a $2,000 to $5,000 payment reduction depending on the HHRG category and the visit type. For a small agency with 15 to 30 active patients, a pattern of unintended LUPA episodes can meaningfully affect monthly cash flow.

The challenge is that LUPA episodes are often not obvious in real time. An agency's billing software may not flag that a 30-day period is approaching a LUPA threshold — especially if visits are being scheduled by clinicians who aren't thinking about payment implications.

LUPA Is Triggered at the 30-Day Period Level

Under PDGM (Patient-Driven Groupings Model), Medicare pays for home health in 30-day periods, not 60-day episodes. Each 30-day period has its own LUPA threshold based on the patient's HHRG for that period. A patient with a complex HHRG in the first 30 days may have a different LUPA threshold than in the second 30 days.

What Changed in 2026

CMS recalibrated LUPA thresholds across HHRG categories for CY 2026. This recalibration was based on updated home health utilization data and is intended to better reflect actual visit patterns for each patient classification.

In practical terms, this means:

  • Some HHRG categories now have higher LUPA thresholds than before — meaning agencies need to deliver more visits to avoid a LUPA in those categories
  • Some categories have lower thresholds — providing a small buffer for agencies that were previously borderline
  • Agencies that were safely above the LUPA threshold in 2025 may be at or below the threshold in 2026 without changing their visit patterns at all

⚠️ The Threshold Moved — Your Visit Pattern Didn't

The most common way agencies get caught by the 2026 LUPA recalibration is simple: they're delivering the same number of visits they always have, but the threshold moved up. What was a safely compliant visit pattern in 2025 may now be triggering LUPAs in certain HHRG categories.

How to Find Out If Your Agency Is at Risk

The only way to know whether your agency is exposed to the 2026 LUPA recalibration is to compare your actual visit patterns — by HHRG category and 30-day period — against the updated thresholds. Here's how to approach this:

Step 1: Pull your HHRG distribution

Ask your billing software or clearinghouse for a report showing the HHRG classifications for your active patients over the last 90 days. This will tell you which HHRG categories make up your patient population.

Step 2: Look up the 2026 LUPA thresholds for your HHRGs

CMS publishes the LUPA thresholds for each HHRG category in the Final Rule and associated payment tables. Your billing software vendor or MAC (Medicare Administrative Contractor) can also provide this information.

Step 3: Compare your visit counts to the thresholds

For each 30-day period in your sample, compare the actual visits delivered against the LUPA threshold for that patient's HHRG. Flag any periods where you came within one or two visits of the threshold.

Step 4: Review your scheduling processes

If you're finding borderline cases, review your visit scheduling processes. Are clinicians aware of LUPA thresholds? Is there a mechanism to flag episodes approaching a LUPA threshold before the period ends?

Example: How a Single Visit Can Change Everything

Imagine a patient classified in an HHRG category with a 2026 LUPA threshold of 4 visits per 30-day period. Your clinician delivers 3 visits in the period — perhaps because the patient cancelled one appointment and it wasn't rescheduled. The full episode rate for this patient might be $1,800. Under LUPA, you receive three per-visit payments of roughly $150–$200 each — approximately $450–$600 total. That's a $1,200 to $1,350 payment reduction on a single period, from one missed visit.

Legitimate Clinical Considerations vs. LUPA Gaming

It's important to draw a clear line here: the goal is not to artificially inflate visit counts to avoid LUPAs. Delivering visits that aren't clinically necessary is a compliance violation that creates its own significant risk — including fraud and abuse exposure.

The goal is to ensure that clinically appropriate visits are being delivered and that visit scheduling processes aren't inadvertently falling below thresholds due to administrative gaps — cancelled visits that aren't rescheduled, discharge decisions made before the end of a 30-day period, or scheduling patterns that don't account for LUPA thresholds.

Questions to Ask Your Billing Team

  • Does our billing software alert us when an active patient is approaching a LUPA threshold?
  • What was our LUPA rate in the last 6 months, and what HHRG categories drove it?
  • Has our LUPA rate changed since January 1, 2026?
  • Do our clinicians know what LUPA is and how it affects reimbursement?
