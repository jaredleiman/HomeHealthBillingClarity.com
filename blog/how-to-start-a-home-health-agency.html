How to Start a Home Health Agency: Step-by-Step Guide

By Home Health Billing Clarity · April 2026 · 10 min read

Starting a home health agency is one of the most complex healthcare businesses to launch — but also one of the most rewarding. The aging population, the shift toward home-based care, and Medicare's zero-copay home health benefit create strong demand. However, the regulatory requirements are substantial. Here's a practical guide to what's involved.

Step 1: Understand the Regulatory Landscape

Home health agencies operate in one of the most heavily regulated segments of healthcare. Before investing any money, understand that you will need state licensing (requirements vary significantly by state), Medicare certification if you plan to serve Medicare patients (most agencies do), compliance with CMS Conditions of Participation (42 CFR Part 484), accreditation from an approved accrediting organization (optional but recommended), and HIPAA compliance infrastructure.

Some states have certificate of need (CON) requirements that limit the number of home health agencies that can operate in a given area. Check your state's requirements before proceeding — if a CON is required and none are available, you may not be able to open in your desired location.

Step 2: Create a Business Plan

Your business plan should address the market analysis showing demand for home health in your service area, your payer mix strategy (Medicare, Medicaid, commercial, private pay), startup capital requirements which typically range from 50,000 to 00,000 depending on state and scale, staffing plan including clinical staff (RNs, therapists) and administrative staff, technology infrastructure including EMR and billing systems, and your timeline for achieving break-even (typically 12-24 months for new agencies).

Step 3: Obtain State Licensure

Every state requires home health agencies to hold a state license. The application process typically requires submitting a detailed application with business information and ownership disclosure, passing a state survey of your physical office and operational policies, demonstrating that you have qualified clinical leadership (a registered nurse as Director of Nursing/Clinical Manager), and maintaining required insurance coverage (professional liability, general liability, workers' compensation).

State licensure timelines range from 2 to 12 months depending on the state. Some states have significantly more complex requirements than others.

Step 4: Apply for Medicare Certification

Medicare certification is the most critical step if you plan to serve Medicare patients. The process includes submitting a CMS-855A enrollment application through PECOS, passing an initial certification survey conducted by the state survey agency on behalf of CMS, demonstrating compliance with all Medicare Conditions of Participation, and establishing an acceptable track record of patient care during the survey.

The Medicare certification process typically takes 6-12 months from application to approval. During this period, you cannot bill Medicare — so you'll need sufficient capital to cover operating costs before Medicare revenue begins.

Critical timing note: You must be actively providing patient care (to non-Medicare patients) before the certification survey can be conducted. CMS surveyors need to observe actual care delivery and review real patient records. Plan to serve Medicaid, commercial insurance, or private-pay patients during the pre-certification period.

Step 5: Build Your Team

At minimum, you need a Director of Nursing / Clinical Manager (RN with home health experience), a qualified administrator, field nurses (RNs and LPNs), therapists (PT, OT, SLP) either employed or contracted, home health aides, a billing and coding specialist familiar with PDGM, and an intake coordinator. Recruiting experienced home health clinicians is often the biggest operational challenge for new agencies. Offering competitive pay, flexible scheduling, and a manageable caseload helps attract talent in a competitive market.

Step 6: Implement Technology

Modern home health agencies require an electronic medical record (EMR) system that supports OASIS documentation, a billing system that handles PDGM claims and manages RAPs, a scheduling and visit verification system (often GPS-enabled), a telephony or telehealth platform for patient monitoring, and a secure communication system for care coordination.

Choose an EMR that is specifically designed for home health — general healthcare EMRs typically cannot handle OASIS documentation, PDGM billing, or home health-specific workflows.

Step 7: Establish Referral Relationships

Home health agencies receive patients through referrals from hospitals (discharge planning departments), physician practices, skilled nursing facilities, and community organizations. Building relationships with referral sources in your service area is essential for patient volume. Focus initially on hospital discharge planners — they are the highest-volume referral source for most home health agencies.

Financial Realities

Home health agencies typically operate on net margins of 3-8% once established. The biggest cost driver is clinical labor (60-70% of revenue). Key financial metrics to track include cost per visit, revenue per episode, visit utilization per episode, and rehospitalization rate (which affects HHVBP scores and future reimbursement).

Most new agencies take 18-24 months to reach break-even and 3-5 years to reach full operational maturity.

The Bottom Line

Starting a home health agency is a significant undertaking requiring substantial capital, regulatory expertise, and clinical leadership. The demand is real and growing, but the barriers to entry exist for good reason — home health agencies care for medically complex, vulnerable patients in unsupervised settings. If you're committed to the compliance and quality requirements, home health can be a sustainable and impactful business. If you're looking for a quick or easy entry into healthcare, this is not it.
