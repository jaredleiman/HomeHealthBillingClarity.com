Does Medicare Cover Home Health Aides? What's Included

By Home Health Billing Clarity · April 2026 · 7 min read

Yes — Medicare covers home health aide services. But there is a critical condition: home health aide coverage is only available when the patient is also receiving a qualifying skilled service (skilled nursing, physical therapy, or speech-language pathology). A home health aide alone, without an accompanying skilled service, is not a covered Medicare benefit.

What Home Health Aides Do

Home health aides provide hands-on personal care assistance under the supervision of a registered nurse. Covered aide services include bathing and showering assistance, dressing and grooming help, hair care and oral hygiene, assistance with toileting and incontinence care, skin care (non-skilled, such as lotion application), assistance with ambulation and transfers, light meal preparation related to the patient's dietary needs, and medication reminders (not administration — that is a skilled nursing task).

The Skilled Service Requirement

This is the most important rule to understand. Medicare views home health aide services as a support to skilled care, not a standalone benefit. The logic is that if a patient needs only personal care assistance (bathing, dressing) without any skilled medical need, they need custodial care — which Medicare does not cover.

In practice, this means a patient receiving skilled nursing for wound care can also receive home health aide visits for bathing assistance. But when the wound heals and skilled nursing is discontinued, the home health aide coverage also stops — even if the patient still needs help bathing.

What families often expect vs. reality: Many families expect Medicare to provide a home health aide for several hours a day, several days a week, to help with daily activities. In practice, Medicare-covered aide visits are typically 1-2 hours, 2-3 times per week, and are tied to the skilled care plan. For families needing more extensive daily assistance, private-pay home care or Medicaid waiver programs are the appropriate options.

Aide Supervision Requirements

Medicare requires that home health aides be supervised by a registered nurse. The supervision requirements include an RN supervisory visit to the patient's home at least once every 14 days when aide services are provided, the RN must be present during the aide visit to observe the aide's performance, and the aide's care plan must be reviewed and updated by the supervising RN as the patient's condition changes.

These supervision visits are separately billable and are critical for compliance. Missing or late aide supervision visits are a common survey deficiency for home health agencies.

How Many Hours Does Medicare Cover?

Medicare does not specify a maximum number of aide hours per week. The plan of care determines the frequency — typically 2-3 visits per week at 1-2 hours each. Under PDGM, aide visits are included in the bundled episode payment, so the agency determines the appropriate visit frequency based on patient need and the plan of care.

Important: home health aide visits are intermittent, just like skilled visits. Medicare does not cover 8-hour aide shifts, overnight aide care, or live-in aide arrangements. Those services require private payment or Medicaid coverage.

When Aide Coverage Ends

Home health aide coverage ends when skilled services are no longer being provided — either because the patient has met their skilled care goals or because they no longer qualify for home health. There is one important exception: occupational therapy can sustain home health eligibility (including aide services) after skilled nursing and other therapies have been discontinued, as long as OT was established while another qualifying skilled service was active.

Alternatives When Medicare Aide Coverage Isn't Enough

  • Medicaid home and community-based services (HCBS) — Many states offer Medicaid waiver programs that provide more extensive aide services, including full-day and live-in care, for patients who meet income and functional eligibility criteria
  • Veterans Aid and Attendance — Veterans and surviving spouses may qualify for a VA pension benefit that helps pay for aide services
  • Long-term care insurance — Policies that cover home care can supplement or replace Medicare aide benefits
  • Private-pay home care agencies — Families can hire aides directly through non-Medicare agencies for additional hours beyond what Medicare covers

The Bottom Line

Medicare covers home health aides as part of a comprehensive home health care plan — but only when skilled services are active. For patients who need daily personal care assistance without a skilled medical need, other programs and payment options are available. For providers, proper aide supervision documentation is essential for survey compliance and audit readiness.
