How to Qualify for Home Health Care Under Medicare

By Home Health Billing Clarity · April 2026 · 8 min read

Qualifying for Medicare home health care comes down to four specific requirements. Meeting all four means you receive comprehensive skilled care at home with zero out-of-pocket cost. Missing even one means Medicare will not cover the services. Here is exactly what each requirement means and how to demonstrate that you qualify.

Requirement 1: You Must Be Homebound

The homebound requirement is the most frequently misunderstood — and the most commonly cited reason for Medicare home health claim denials. "Homebound" does not mean you cannot leave your home. It means that leaving home requires considerable and taxing effort due to your medical condition.

You meet the homebound standard if you need the help of another person to leave home (assistance walking, wheelchair transport), you need special medical equipment to leave (walker, cane, oxygen, wheelchair), your condition makes leaving inadvisable (fall risk, cognitive impairment, post-surgical restrictions), or leaving home requires a major effort that most people in normal health would not need.

You can still qualify as homebound even if you leave home for medical appointments and treatment, attend religious services, go on occasional outings of short duration (a family dinner, a haircut), or attend an adult day care program.

What disqualifies homebound status: regularly leaving home for non-medical activities that suggest your condition does not significantly restrict your ability to leave, such as driving to work, shopping regularly, or maintaining an active social schedule.

For providers: Homebound documentation is the #1 audit target in home health. Every OASIS assessment and recertification must include a specific, individualized description of WHY this patient is homebound — not a generic statement. "Patient is homebound due to severe COPD with O2 dependency, requiring assistance to ambulate to bathroom, unable to climb the 4 stairs to exit home without rest breaks and caregiver support" is audit-proof. "Patient is homebound" is not.

Requirement 2: You Must Need Skilled Care

Medicare requires that you need at least one skilled service: skilled nursing care provided on an intermittent basis, physical therapy, or speech-language pathology. Once one of these skilled services qualifies you, occupational therapy, medical social services, and home health aide services can be added to your care plan.

"Skilled" means the service requires the knowledge and judgment of a licensed professional. A nurse assessing a wound, adjusting medication dosages, or managing a PICC line is providing skilled care. Someone helping you get dressed or preparing meals is providing custodial care — which alone does not qualify for Medicare home health.

Requirement 3: You Must Have a Physician's Order

A physician, nurse practitioner, or physician assistant must order your home health services and establish a plan of care. The ordering provider must also certify that you meet the homebound and skilled care requirements. Additionally, a face-to-face encounter must occur within specific timeframes — the provider must have seen you in person to confirm the need for home health services.

The face-to-face encounter must occur within 90 days before or 30 days after the start of home health services. The encounter documentation must describe the clinical condition that supports homebound status and the need for skilled services.

Requirement 4: Medicare-Certified Agency

Services must be delivered by a home health agency that holds Medicare certification. Certification means the agency has met CMS's Conditions of Participation, passed a state survey, and is approved to bill Medicare. You can verify an agency's certification through Medicare's Home Health Compare tool at medicare.gov.

What to Do If You Think You Qualify

  1. Talk to your doctor — Explain your limitations and ask whether home health services are appropriate. If you've recently been hospitalized or had surgery, the hospital discharge planner can initiate the referral.
  2. Request specific services — If you need wound care, ask for skilled nursing. If you need rehabilitation, ask for physical or occupational therapy. Be specific about what you need help with.
  3. Choose an agency — You have the right to choose any Medicare-certified agency in your area. Compare quality ratings on medicare.gov's Home Health Compare.
  4. Be honest about your limitations — During the intake assessment, describe your actual daily functioning. If walking to the kitchen exhausts you, say so. If you need help getting in and out of the shower, say so. The assessment determines your eligibility and your care plan.

What If You're Denied?

If Medicare denies your home health claim, you have the right to appeal. Common denial reasons include insufficient documentation of homebound status (the fix is better documentation from your physician, not necessarily a change in your condition), no qualifying skilled need (if your only need is custodial care, Medicare home health is not the right benefit — but you may qualify for Medicaid home care or other programs), and missing face-to-face encounter documentation (the physician needs to complete the required encounter documentation).

The Bottom Line

If you're recovering from surgery, managing a serious illness, or living with a condition that makes leaving home difficult, you likely qualify for Medicare home health care — and it costs you nothing out of pocket. The key is meeting all four requirements, particularly the homebound standard, and having a physician who documents your eligibility clearly. Don't assume you don't qualify — talk to your doctor.
