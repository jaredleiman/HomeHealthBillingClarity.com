How Long Will Medicare Pay for Home Health Care?

By Home Health Billing Clarity · April 2026 · 7 min read

There is no fixed time limit on Medicare home health care. Medicare will continue to cover home health services as long as you meet the eligibility criteria — homebound status, need for skilled care, physician certification, and care from a certified agency. Some patients receive home health for a few weeks after surgery; others receive it for years while managing chronic conditions.

The 60-Day Certification Period

Medicare home health is authorized in 60-day certification periods, each consisting of two 30-day episodes. At the start of each 60-day period, the patient's physician must sign a certification (or recertification) confirming that the patient continues to meet all eligibility requirements, the services ordered are medically necessary, and a plan of care has been established or reviewed.

At the end of each 60-day period, the agency can request recertification for another 60 days — and this process can repeat indefinitely as long as the patient qualifies.

What Keeps Coverage Going

Continued Medicare home health coverage depends on three things being true at each recertification:

  • Homebound status persists — The patient must still meet the homebound definition. If a patient recovers enough mobility that leaving home is no longer a considerable effort, homebound status ends and so does coverage.
  • Skilled need continues — The patient must still require at least one qualifying skilled service. When all skilled care goals have been met and no further skilled intervention is needed, coverage ends.
  • Progress or maintenance justification — The patient must be making progress toward treatment goals OR the skilled services must be necessary to maintain the patient's current condition and prevent decline. The Jimmo v. Sebelius settlement (2013) clarified that Medicare cannot deny coverage solely because a patient isn't improving — maintenance of function by skilled professionals is a valid covered service.

Jimmo v. Sebelius impact: Before this settlement, many Medicare claims were denied with "no improvement" rationale. The settlement clarified that skilled nursing and therapy services needed to maintain a patient's condition or prevent deterioration are covered, even if the patient isn't expected to improve. This is particularly important for patients with progressive conditions like ALS, MS, or advanced COPD.

Common Reasons Coverage Ends

  • Patient is no longer homebound — Recovery improves mobility to the point where leaving home is no longer a considerable effort
  • Skilled care goals met — The wound has healed, the patient has completed rehabilitation, or medication management has been stabilized
  • Patient is hospitalized — Home health coverage pauses during inpatient stays and a new episode begins upon discharge if the patient requalifies
  • Patient declines services — Patients can refuse home health at any time
  • Patient transitions to hospice — When a patient elects hospice care, home health services related to the terminal diagnosis transition to the hospice benefit

What Happens Between Episodes

If there is a gap between certification periods — for example, the patient is discharged from home health and then re-referred three months later — a new start of care OASIS assessment is required, a new face-to-face encounter must occur, and the episode is classified as a new admission (which affects PDGM payment). There is no penalty for having a gap, but the full eligibility documentation process starts over.

For Providers: Recertification Best Practices

The recertification process is where many agencies face compliance risk. Best practices include beginning the recertification process 5-7 days before the end of the current 60-day period, updating the OASIS assessment to reflect current functional status and care needs, documenting specific reasons why continued skilled care is necessary with individualized clinical rationale, ensuring the physician reviews and signs the recertification and updated plan of care before the new period begins, and tracking recertification timelines proactively rather than reacting to expirations.

Late or missing recertifications result in claim denials for visits provided after the certification period expires. This is preventable with a reliable tracking system.

The Bottom Line

Medicare will pay for home health care as long as you qualify — there is no fixed time limit, no maximum number of episodes, and no annual cap. The practical limit is your eligibility: the day you no longer need skilled care or are no longer homebound is the day coverage ends. For patients with chronic or progressive conditions, this means home health can be a long-term resource. For providers, it means each recertification must be treated as a fresh eligibility determination with documentation that stands on its own.
