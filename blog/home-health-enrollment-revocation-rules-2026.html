Home Health Enrollment and Revocation Rules 2026: What Independent Agencies Must Know

Published April 2026  ·  HomeHealthBillingClarity.com

Of all the changes in the CY 2026 CMS Home Health Final Rule, the enrollment and revocation changes carry the highest downside risk for independent agencies. A revocation of Medicare billing privileges isn't a fine or a payment reduction — it's a potential end to your ability to bill Medicare at all. Here's what changed and what your agency needs to do.

🛡️ Illustration: Medicare enrollment certificate protected by a shield, with a 30-day calendar — sage green editorial style

Why Enrollment Compliance Is Existential for Small Agencies

For an independent home health agency whose revenue is predominantly or entirely Medicare, a revocation of Medicare billing privileges is catastrophic. Unlike a billing error that generates a demand letter or an overpayment that gets recouped over time, a revocation can stop your ability to bill Medicare — and collect Medicare revenue — almost immediately.

Small independent agencies are particularly vulnerable to enrollment compliance issues because they typically don't have dedicated compliance staff monitoring their enrollment status, responding to CMS correspondence, and tracking reporting deadlines. These responsibilities often fall to the agency owner or administrator who is managing dozens of other operational priorities simultaneously.

The Two Major 2026 Changes

Change 1: Adverse Action Reporting Window Shortened from 90 to 30 Days

Before 2026, Medicare-certified home health agencies were required to self-report "adverse actions" to CMS within 90 days of the agency knowing or having reason to know about the adverse action. The 2026 Final Rule cut that window to 30 calendar days.

What Counts as an "Adverse Action"?

An adverse action, for reporting purposes, includes:

  • Any final adverse action taken against the agency, a principal, or a managing employee
  • Exclusions from Medicare, Medicaid, or other federal health care programs
  • License revocations or suspensions
  • Conviction of a criminal offense related to the delivery of health care items or services
  • Civil monetary penalties or exclusions imposed by the OIG

The definition is broader than many operators realize. It includes adverse actions against individuals employed by or associated with the agency — not just actions taken against the agency as an entity.

Change 2: Expanded Retroactive Revocation Authority

The 2026 rule also expanded CMS's authority to revoke a provider's Medicare billing privileges retroactively — meaning CMS can reach back in time and revoke privileges from a date earlier than the revocation notice itself.

The practical consequence of retroactive revocation is significant: claims that were already paid during the retroactive period become subject to recoupment. Medicare can seek to recover payments made for services billed during the retroactive revocation period, even if those services were otherwise appropriate and well-documented.

🚨 What Retroactive Revocation Can Mean in Practice

Imagine your agency has a managing employee who received a license suspension six months ago — an adverse action that wasn't reported to CMS within 30 days because no one in the agency knew it triggered a reporting requirement. CMS discovers this during an enrollment review.

Under expanded retroactive revocation authority, CMS could:

  • Revoke your agency's Medicare billing privileges effective from the date of the license suspension
  • Seek to recoup six months of Medicare payments made after that date
  • Impose a re-enrollment bar that prevents your agency from re-enrolling in Medicare for up to three years

This scenario is not theoretical — it represents the kind of cascading consequence that the expanded retroactive revocation authority enables.

The 30-Day Clock: How It Works

The 30-day adverse action reporting window runs from the date the agency knew or should have known about the adverse action — not the date CMS formally notified the agency, and not the date the agency decided to report it.

This distinction is critical. If an internal HR process surfaces information about a managing employee's license issue, the 30-day clock starts at the point the agency had access to that information — even if it wasn't formally escalated or flagged as a reportable event.

Day 0: Adverse action occurs or becomes known

Clock starts. Could be a license suspension notice, an OIG exclusion, a CMS correspondence, or an internal audit finding.

Days 1–15: Evaluate and prepare

Determine whether the event meets the definition of an adverse action. Consult your compliance advisor or attorney if uncertain. Begin preparing your self-report documentation.

Days 15–28: Submit self-report

Submit through PECOS (Provider Enrollment, Chain, and Ownership System) or as directed by your MAC. Build in time for technical issues and confirmation.

Day 30: Hard deadline

Failure to self-report by this date is itself a compliance violation that can be used to support a revocation action.

What Your Agency Needs to Put in Place

The 30-day window is tight enough that you cannot handle adverse action reporting reactively. You need a proactive monitoring and response process. At minimum, this means:

  1. Designate an adverse action point person. Someone in your agency needs to own this — a specific named individual who is responsible for monitoring, evaluating, and reporting adverse actions within 30 days.
  2. Set up OIG exclusion monitoring. The OIG publishes monthly updates to its exclusion database. Your point person should check this monthly for all principals, managing employees, and contracted clinical staff.
  3. Create an internal intake process. Staff need a way to report potential adverse actions to the point person. This doesn't need to be elaborate — a dedicated email address or a standing agenda item in a weekly meeting is sufficient.
  4. Know your PECOS login. You should be able to log into PECOS and submit an enrollment update at any time. If you don't know your login credentials, resolve this now — not when you're 20 days into a 30-day window.
  5. Document everything. Keep a written record of every adverse action evaluation — including events you concluded did not require reporting and your reasoning. This documentation protects you if CMS later questions your response to a particular event.

Re-Enrollment Bars: The Long-Term Consequence

When CMS revokes a provider's Medicare billing privileges, it also typically imposes a re-enrollment bar — a period during which the provider cannot re-apply for Medicare enrollment. For home health agencies, re-enrollment bars of one to three years are common in revocation situations involving compliance violations.

For a small independent agency whose revenue is primarily Medicare, a three-year re-enrollment bar is not a recoverable event. It's a business-ending consequence. This is why enrollment compliance deserves to be treated as the highest-priority compliance area in your agency.
