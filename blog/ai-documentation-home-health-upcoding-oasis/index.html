AI Documentation Tools and Home Health Upcoding: What OASIS Reviewers Are Catching

The Blue Cross Blue Shield Association's March 2026 report on AI-driven hospital upcoding pegged the national cost at $2.3 billion across inpatient and outpatient claims. Home health wasn't in the BCBS sample, but it should have been. The Patient-Driven Groupings Model (PDGM) makes home health one of the most coding-sensitive settings in all of Medicare, and the AI tools agencies have adopted to keep up with OASIS-E are producing exactly the kind of coding drift payer AI is built to detect.

Why Home Health Is Uniquely Exposed

PDGM pays on clinical grouping, comorbidity adjustment, and functional impairment — all of which are determined by OASIS and ICD-10 coding at the start of care. Get a comorbidity wrong and you move from a low-weight clinical grouping to a high one. Get a functional item wrong and your case-mix weight changes materially. The incentive to code to the highest defensible level is structural.

AI-assisted OASIS tools promise to solve the documentation bottleneck. They read the clinician's notes, scan the hospital discharge summary and physician orders, and suggest M-item responses and ICD-10 codes. When clinicians accept the suggestions without careful review, the same pattern BCBS documented in hospitals appears in home health: diagnoses coded without corresponding care delivered.

Where AI Drift Shows Up on OASIS

  • M1021/M1023 primary and other diagnoses. AI tools pull every diagnosis from the discharge summary and rank them for case-mix impact. Agencies that accept suggested diagnoses without verifying each one is active, being treated, and supported by the comprehensive assessment are submitting OASIS data that won't survive medical review.
  • M1800 series grooming/dressing/bathing. AI-generated responses tend to trend toward higher dependency levels, which carry higher functional points. HHH MAC reviewers are pulling charts where M1810 (dressing upper body) is coded "3 - unable to dress" but the visit note documents the patient dressing themselves in the therapist's presence.
  • M1033 risk for hospitalization. Case-mix impactful. AI tools flag risk factors liberally. Reviewers expect every selected risk factor to be specifically identified in the clinical narrative.
  • Comorbidity adjustment groups. The comorbidity adjustment in PDGM requires the secondary diagnosis to meet specific interaction criteria with the primary. AI tools often suggest comorbidities that add case-mix weight but don't meet the interaction threshold. These are first to be stripped in pre-payment review.

What HHH MACs and UPICs Are Already Doing

The Targeted Probe and Educate (TPE) program has been active against home health agencies with outlier billing patterns for years. What changed in 2025 is the sensitivity of the targeting. CGS, Palmetto GBA, and National Government Services are all using claims analytics that identify agencies with case-mix weights materially higher than their geographic peers — and the lookback window is now as short as 60 days.

UPICs (Unified Program Integrity Contractors) target fraud-level anomalies, but the threshold has come down. An agency that moved from a $2,100 average case-mix weight to $2,800 in six months following EMR AI adoption is exactly the kind of pattern that triggers a medical records request now — not two years from now.

A Five-Chart Self-Audit You Can Run This Week

  1. Pull five OASIS SOC assessments from the last 60 days.
  2. For each, check that every M1021/M1023 diagnosis is active, being treated on the plan of care, and present in the comprehensive assessment narrative.
  3. Verify that M1800 functional items match what the visit notes actually show — not what the hospital discharge summary predicted.
  4. Confirm M1033 risk factors are specifically called out in the clinical narrative, not just checkboxed.
  5. Check that any comorbidity-interaction diagnoses meet the PDGM interaction criteria for your primary grouping.

If you find drift in one chart out of five, you have a review-workflow problem. If you find it in two or more, you have an agency-level problem that should be addressed before your next TPE sample.

The Bottom Line

BCBS identified $2.3 billion in hospital AI upcoding before any agency enforcement body published comparable home health numbers. The gap isn't because home health is safer — it's because the data analysis takes longer in a fee-for-service-adjacent model. When those numbers publish, the agencies that built a genuine human review step between AI-suggested OASIS responses and final submission will look very different from the agencies that didn't.
