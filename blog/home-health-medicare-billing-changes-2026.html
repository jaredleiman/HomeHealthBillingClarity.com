Home Health Medicare Billing Changes 2026: What Independent Agencies Need to Know

Published April 2026  ·  HomeHealthBillingClarity.com

The CY 2026 CMS Home Health Final Rule took effect January 1, 2026 — and it changed more than any single rule update in recent memory. If you run an independent home health agency, this article breaks down what changed, what it means for your billing, and where your highest risk areas are.

📊 Illustration: 2025 vs. 2026 home health billing rule comparison timeline — sage green and gold editorial style

Why 2026 Is Different

CMS doesn't overhaul home health billing rules every year. Most annual updates involve minor payment rate adjustments and technical corrections. The 2026 Final Rule is different — it introduces structural changes to how agencies document care, how they bill for visits, how they maintain their Medicare enrollment, and how they're evaluated for value-based performance.

For large home health chains with dedicated compliance departments, these changes are manageable. For independent agencies — especially those with 5 to 30 staff running on manual billing processes — the 2026 rules represent real exposure if left unaddressed.

The Five Major Changes

1. OASIS All-Payer Reporting High Impact

Starting in 2026, agencies are required to submit OASIS assessments for all patients — not just Medicare and Medicaid patients. This all-payer expansion means your documentation burden increases regardless of how your payer mix is structured.

For agencies that previously only completed OASIS for their Medicare patients, this is a significant operational change. Every patient admission, recertification, and discharge now requires a compliant OASIS assessment.

2. LUPA Threshold Recalibration Medium Impact

Low Utilization Payment Adjustment (LUPA) thresholds — the minimum visit counts required to receive full episode payment — have been recalibrated across Home Health Resource Group (HHRG) categories. Some thresholds went up; some went down.

If your agency's visit patterns fall near any LUPA threshold, you may be at risk of triggering a LUPA episode and receiving significantly reduced reimbursement. This requires review of your visit scheduling patterns by HHRG category.

3. Face-to-Face Encounter Rule Changes Medium Impact

The 2026 rule expanded the list of practitioners who are eligible to conduct and document face-to-face encounters for home health certification. Nurse practitioners, clinical nurse specialists, and certified nurse midwives now have expanded eligibility in certain circumstances.

While this expansion adds flexibility, it also creates documentation risk — agencies need to ensure their face-to-face records accurately reflect the practitioner's eligibility status at the time of the encounter.

4. Enrollment and Revocation Changes High Impact

This is the highest-risk change in the 2026 Final Rule for independent agencies. CMS made two significant enforcement changes:

  • Adverse action reporting window shortened from 90 to 30 days. If your agency experiences an audit finding, compliance violation, or substantiated beneficiary complaint, you now have 30 calendar days — not 90 — to self-report to CMS.
  • Expanded retroactive revocation authority. CMS can now revoke a provider's Medicare billing privileges retroactively, meaning claims already paid can be recouped if an enrollment violation is later discovered.

5. HHVBP Model Expansion Medium Impact

The Home Health Value-Based Purchasing (HHVBP) model — which adjusts Medicare payments based on quality performance — now applies to all 50 states. Previously limited to a demonstration group, every Medicare-participating home health agency is now subject to HHVBP scoring and payment adjustments.

What This Means for Small Independent Agencies

Large chains have compliance departments that track these changes and update processes proactively. Independent agencies typically don't — which means the risk of being caught off-guard is much higher. The 30-day adverse action window in particular leaves very little margin for error.

The good news: none of these changes require expensive consultants to understand. They require clear information about what the rules say and how your specific situation compares.

Where to Start

If you haven't already reviewed your agency's processes against the 2026 rules, the place to start is enrollment compliance — specifically, confirming that you have a process in place to identify and self-report adverse actions within 30 days. This is the change with the most severe downside risk.

From there, review your OASIS documentation workflows for all-payer compliance, and pull a report of your visit patterns by HHRG to check your LUPA exposure.
