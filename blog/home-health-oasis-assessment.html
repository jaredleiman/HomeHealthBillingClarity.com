Home Health OASIS Assessment: What Providers Need to Know

By Home Health Billing Clarity · April 2026 · 8 min read

OASIS — the Outcome and Assessment Information Set — is the standardized patient assessment tool required by CMS for all Medicare and Medicaid home health patients. It is simultaneously a clinical assessment, a payment determinant, a quality measurement tool, and an audit target. For home health agencies, OASIS accuracy directly affects reimbursement, quality scores, and compliance risk.

What OASIS Measures

The current version (OASIS-E1) collects data across multiple domains including patient demographics and history, living situation, risk factors (falls, hospitalization, pressure ulcers), sensory status (vision, hearing, pain), skin integrity (wound assessment), respiratory status, cardiac status, elimination status, neuro/emotional/behavioral status, functional abilities and goals (GG items covering self-care and mobility), and medication management.

The functional items (Section GG) are particularly important under PDGM because they directly determine the patient's functional impairment level, which is one of the four factors that sets the episode payment amount.

When OASIS Assessments Are Required

OASIS assessments must be completed at specific points during the patient's care:

  • Start of Care (SOC) — Within 5 calendar days of the first billable home health visit
  • Resumption of Care (ROC) — Within 5 days of the patient returning from an inpatient stay
  • Recertification — Within the last 5 days of each 60-day certification period
  • Other Follow-Up — When there is a significant change in condition
  • Transfer to Inpatient Facility — When the patient is admitted to a hospital or other inpatient facility
  • Discharge — When the patient is discharged from home health services

Each assessment type has a specific OASIS time point and data submission deadline. Late submissions can result in payment penalties.

How OASIS Affects PDGM Payment

Under PDGM, the OASIS assessment determines two of the four payment variables:

  • Clinical grouping — The primary diagnosis coded on the OASIS determines which of 12 clinical groups the episode falls into
  • Functional impairment level — Section GG scores on self-care and mobility items determine the patient's functional level (low, medium, or high impairment)

Accurate OASIS scoring is therefore a direct revenue driver. Under-scoring functional impairment results in lower payment. Over-scoring creates audit and compliance risk. The goal is accuracy — documenting the patient's actual functional status at the time of assessment.

OASIS accuracy = financial accuracy: A study by CMS found that inaccurate OASIS scoring costs the average home health agency 3-5% of revenue annually through under-coding. At the same time, over-coding triggers targeted audits. Investing in OASIS training and quality review pays for itself through accurate reimbursement and reduced audit risk.

Common OASIS Errors

  • Scoring functional items based on what the patient CAN do vs. what they ACTUALLY do — OASIS GG items ask about the patient's usual performance, not their best performance. A patient who can walk 50 feet with a walker but usually uses a wheelchair should be scored based on usual performance.
  • Inconsistent scoring between SOC and discharge — Quality measures are calculated by comparing SOC and discharge scores. If the SOC scores are artificially high (under-documenting impairment), the improvement gap will appear small, hurting your quality scores.
  • Missing wound assessments — OASIS M-items for wounds must be completed with specific measurements and characteristics. Incomplete wound documentation is a top survey deficiency.
  • Late submissions — OASIS data must be transmitted to CMS within 30 days of the assessment date. Late transmissions trigger payment penalties.

OASIS Best Practices for Agencies

The agencies with the best OASIS accuracy and quality scores follow consistent practices. They designate trained OASIS clinicians rather than having every nurse complete assessments, conduct internal OASIS reviews before submission to catch coding errors, use the OASIS guidance manual for every disputed item rather than relying on memory, track OASIS completion timelines to avoid late submissions, and compare their OASIS scores to national benchmarks to identify potential under- or over-coding patterns.
