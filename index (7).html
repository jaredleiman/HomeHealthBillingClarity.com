PDGM Downcoding: How Medicare Advantage AI Is Quietly Cutting Home Health Reimbursement

11 min read · For independent home health agency owners and administrators
Quick Answer

Medicare Advantage plans — which now cover roughly half of all Medicare beneficiaries — have deployed AI claim-review systems that silently downcode home health 30-day payment periods to lower-paying HHRG case-mix categories. The mechanism reduces the clinical grouping, drops the comorbidity adjustment, lowers the functional impairment level, or reclassifies the admission source — each shift cutting payment by hundreds to thousands of dollars per period without ever denying the claim. The American Academy of Family Physicians called for a federal investigation in 2026, and several states introduced AI downcoding transparency bills. Independent home health agencies can respond by auditing remittance data for case-mix shifts vs submitted OASIS, restructuring documentation to defend each PDGM variable, and submitting batch appeals when systematic patterns emerge.

You complete a Start of Care OASIS assessment on a recently-discharged hospital patient with CHF, COPD, diabetes, and significant functional impairment. Your billing team submits the claim for the first 30-day period. PDGM grouping logic places the patient in an Institutional admission source, MMTA-Cardiac clinical group, with high functional impairment and a high comorbidity adjustment — a high-paying HHRG.

Two weeks later, the MA plan pays the claim. You don't get a denial letter. You get paid. But when your billing team reconciles the payment to the expected case-mix amount, they see the plan paid you as if the patient were in a lower clinical group, with no comorbidity adjustment, and a lower functional level — about $800 less than the HHRG you submitted should have generated.

This is PDGM downcoding by AI, and in 2026 it became one of the largest silent revenue leaks in independent home health.

How PDGM payment actually works

The Patient-Driven Groupings Model groups every 30-day period of home health care into one of 432 case-mix categories, each tied to a specific HHRG and payment amount. Five variables drive the assignment:

Variable What It Captures Payment Impact
Admission source Institutional (from hospital/SNF) vs Community Institutional pays significantly more
Timing Early (first 30 days) vs Late (subsequent periods) Early pays more
Clinical group 12 groups based on principal diagnosis (MMTA-Cardiac, MMTA-Respiratory, Wounds, Behavioral Health, etc.) Wide variation — Wounds and MMTA-Surgical groups pay highest
Functional impairment Low/Medium/High based on M1800-M1860 OASIS items High pays most, three-tier system
Comorbidity adjustment None / Low / High based on secondary diagnoses High adds substantially to base payment

AI downcoding by MA plans typically targets one or more of these variables — most commonly the comorbidity adjustment (dropped to "None" when documentation doesn't surface the secondary diagnoses cleanly), the functional impairment level (downgraded based on inferred OASIS interpretation), or the clinical group (reclassified from MMTA-Cardiac to a lower-paying group when the principal diagnosis coding is questioned).

Why home health is hit harder than most specialties

Three structural factors make home health an unusually frequent AI downcoding target.

OASIS responses are interpreted, not just transmitted. Unlike a CPT code on a CMS-1500 form, OASIS data is rich enough that AI tools can re-evaluate it. If your assessment marks M1860 (ambulation) at level 3 but the AI judges the supporting documentation more consistent with level 1, the system can recalculate the case-mix category and pay accordingly.

The comorbidity adjustment is fragile. The high comorbidity adjustment requires two or more secondary diagnoses from specified comorbidity subgroups, with documented interactions. AI tools frequently flag claims where the secondary diagnoses are present but the documentation doesn't show explicit interaction, dropping the adjustment to "None" — a four-figure swing per 30-day period.

MA penetration grew dramatically. When PDGM launched in 2020, traditional Medicare paid most home health claims. By 2026, MA plans pay roughly half — and MA plans operate with commercial-style claim review, including AI tools. The downcoding pressure is therefore newer and more severe than agencies are tooled to detect.

The Pattern in Plain Numbers

An independent agency with 100 active patients, half on MA plans, billing roughly 200 30-day periods per month between MA patients across two billable periods each, faces material exposure. If the AI downcoding rate on MA periods is 20% and the average shift is $600 per period, that's $24,000 per month, or roughly $290,000 per year in revenue earned but never received. Even a 10% downcoding rate translates to six figures annually.

How to detect AI downcoding in your own agency

Detection requires comparing what you submitted (OASIS data + claim) to what was paid (the HHRG and payment amount on the remittance). Most home health billing systems can produce this comparison if you ask the right way.

The 60-minute audit

  1. Pull your last 90 days of paid 30-day periods from MA plans, separated by payer. Most home health platforms (HCHB, Axxess, WellSky, Alora) export this as a CSV with the submitted HHRG and the paid amount.
  2. For each period, look up what the submitted HHRG should have paid based on the current PDGM rate structure. CMS publishes case-mix weights and base rates annually.
  3. Calculate the variance between expected payment and actual payment. Anything more than minor variance per period is worth investigating.
  4. Group the variance by payer and by PDGM variable. Often one or two MA plans drive the majority of the loss, and one variable (usually comorbidity adjustment) is doing most of the damage.

This audit is more involved than the CPT-to-CPT comparison most specialties run, but the dollar impact per identified pattern is also much higher. A single corrected case-mix category on a high-volume plan can recover thousands per period going forward.

How to document to defend each PDGM variable

The fastest documentation upgrade most agencies can make: build OASIS documentation that explicitly defends each variable driving the case-mix assignment, particularly the comorbidity adjustment and functional impairment level.

Comorbidity adjustment

The high comorbidity adjustment requires two qualifying secondary diagnoses from the CMS-published comorbidity subgroup list, plus documentation that the comorbidities interact with the principal diagnosis or with each other. If your patient has CHF as principal and DM as secondary, document explicitly: "Patient's diabetes is a contributing factor to fluid management and dietary compliance; insulin titration and CHF medication management require coordinated care." That kind of language defends the adjustment against AI re-evaluation.

Functional impairment

M1800-M1860 OASIS items drive the functional level. AI tools cross-reference the OASIS responses against the SOC visit narrative. If M1860 says the patient requires substantial assistance with ambulation but the visit narrative says "patient ambulating with rolling walker independently to the bathroom," the AI flags the inconsistency and downcodes. Train clinicians to write narrative consistent with the OASIS responses, not contradictory.

Clinical group

The principal diagnosis on the claim drives the clinical group. AI tools sometimes flag claims where the principal diagnosis appears inconsistent with the OASIS clinical picture. Defending the clinical group means ensuring the principal diagnosis is the condition driving the home health episode — and that the OASIS narrative supports that.

How to appeal a downcoded HHRG

MA plan appeals require:

  • Documentation supporting each variable — admission source documentation (hospital discharge summary), clinical group justification (principal diagnosis support), functional impairment evidence (OASIS + narrative consistency), and comorbidity interaction documentation.
  • Reference to the MA plan's published medical policy. Most major MA plans have a home health coverage policy. Quote it. Make them prove their AI followed it.
  • Pattern documentation in the cover letter. "Our agency has identified [N] downcoded 30-day periods to [Plan] over the past [period], totaling $[amount] in unrecovered revenue."

Most MA plans have a 60-180 day appeal window from the EOB date. Single-claim appeals carry less leverage than batch appeals — submitting 20+ downcoded periods at once with a systematic-pattern cover letter signals regulatory complaint risk and tends to produce better resolution.

What's coming in 2026

The legislative response is moving but unevenly. Several state bills proposed in 2026 would require payers (including MA plans) to disclose AI involvement in claim adjudication; others would create a private right of action for systematic downcoding. None have become law yet. What's more likely in the next 6-12 months:

  • State insurance commissioner investigations after enough complaints accumulate. Several states have signaled interest.
  • CMS Office of the Inspector General attention. OIG audits MA plan operations regularly, and AI-driven systematic downcoding is the kind of pattern that draws scrutiny.
  • Quiet plan policy revisions. AI thresholds get retuned without announcement when pressure builds. The only reliable detection is monitoring your remittance patterns over time.
  • Class action litigation. Home health trade associations have signaled litigation may follow if administrative action doesn't produce results.

None of this helps an agency getting downcoded today. What helps today is detection, documentation upgrades on each PDGM variable, and batch appeals on systematic patterns.

The bigger picture

AI downcoding by MA plans sits in a larger pattern that hit independent home health agencies in 2026: payers using automation to extract margin without changing the contract or the regulation. It's the mirror image of the AI upcoding stories at large health systems and DRG-aggressive hospitals. Same technology, opposite direction of force. Independent agencies sit in the middle — without the AI tools that large systems use to push codes up, but absorbing the AI tools commercial and MA payers use to push codes down. The only available defense is regular monitoring of your own remittance data — period-by-period, payer-by-payer.

The agencies that catch this in 2026 are the ones treating remittance reconciliation as something to read, not just file.
