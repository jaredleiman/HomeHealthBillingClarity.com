Home Health Skilled Nursing: What Medicare Covers

By Home Health Billing Clarity · April 2026 · 8 min read

Skilled nursing is the most frequently provided home health service and the one that most commonly qualifies patients for the Medicare home health benefit. When a physician orders home health skilled nursing, a registered nurse (RN) or licensed practical nurse (LPN) visits the patient's home to provide medical care that requires professional training and clinical judgment.

What Skilled Nursing Visits Include

Skilled nursing in home health covers a wide range of clinical services. The most common include:

Wound Care

Assessment, cleaning, debridement, dressing changes, and monitoring of surgical wounds, pressure ulcers, diabetic ulcers, and other complex wounds. This is one of the most clear-cut skilled nursing needs — wound care requires clinical assessment and technique that cannot be safely performed by an untrained person.

Medication Management

Reviewing and reconciling medications, assessing for side effects and interactions, educating patients on proper administration, prefilling medication organizers, and monitoring therapeutic response. This is particularly important for patients discharged from the hospital with new or changed medications.

Disease Management Education

Teaching patients and caregivers to manage chronic conditions — diabetes self-management, heart failure symptom monitoring, COPD action plans, anticoagulation management. The skilled component is the clinical assessment of the patient's understanding and ability to self-manage, not just handing over a brochure.

IV Therapy and Infusions

Administering IV antibiotics, IV fluids, parenteral nutrition, or other infusion therapies at home. This includes line maintenance, site assessment, and monitoring for complications.

Assessment and Monitoring

Comprehensive assessment of vital signs, cardiac status, respiratory status, neurological changes, pain levels, fall risk, and overall functional decline. Skilled assessment is particularly important for patients with unstable conditions or those recently discharged from acute care.

Catheter and Ostomy Care

Foley catheter insertion and maintenance, ostomy care and patient education, and monitoring for complications including infection.

How Often Does the Nurse Visit?

Visit frequency is determined by the plan of care based on patient need. Common patterns include daily visits for the first week after hospital discharge to stabilize a complex patient, 2-3 visits per week for active wound care, medication adjustment, or disease management, weekly visits for monitoring and maintenance of stable chronic conditions, and PRN (as needed) visits for condition changes between scheduled visits.

Medicare requires that skilled nursing be provided on an "intermittent" basis — meaning part-time or periodic visits, not continuous care. CMS defines intermittent as up to 28 hours per week of combined skilled nursing and home health aide services, with a maximum of 8 hours in any single day. In practice, most home health nursing visits are 30-60 minutes each.

The "intermittent" vs. "continuous" distinction matters: If a patient needs 24-hour nursing care, they need private-duty nursing — not home health. Private-duty nursing is a separate benefit with different coverage rules and typically requires prior authorization. Home health skilled nursing covers scheduled visits, not shifts.

When Skilled Nursing Is and Isn't Covered

Skilled nursing is covered when the service requires the knowledge and judgment of a licensed nurse. It is not covered when the task could be safely and effectively performed by a non-professional or the patient/caregiver themselves. Medication administration by injection that a caregiver has been trained to perform, simple wound care that a caregiver can manage after instruction, and vital sign monitoring when the patient can reliably self-monitor are examples that may transition from skilled to non-skilled as the patient and caregiver become competent.

The skill is often in the assessment and teaching, not just the task. A nurse visiting to change a simple wound dressing may also be assessing wound healing trajectory, monitoring for infection, evaluating the patient's nutritional status, and adjusting the wound care protocol — all of which are skilled functions.

Documentation Requirements for Providers

Every skilled nursing visit must be documented with the date, time, and duration of the visit, the specific skilled services performed, objective assessment findings (vital signs, wound measurements, lab results), the patient's response to treatment and progress toward goals, changes in condition requiring physician notification, patient and caregiver education provided and their demonstrated understanding, and the plan for next visit including any changes to the plan of care.

The nursing documentation must support two things: that the service was skilled (required a nurse's training and judgment) and that it was medically necessary (connected to the patient's diagnosis and plan of care goals).

The Bottom Line

Skilled nursing is the backbone of Medicare home health care. It qualifies patients for the benefit, opens the door to all other home health services, and addresses the most clinically complex needs in the home setting. For patients, it means professional medical care delivered where you're most comfortable. For providers, it means meticulous documentation of what makes each visit skilled and why the patient continues to need it.
