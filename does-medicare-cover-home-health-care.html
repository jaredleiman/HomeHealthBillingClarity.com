Does Medicare Cover Home Health Care? Complete Guide

By Home Health Billing Clarity · April 2026 · 10 min read

Yes — Medicare covers home health care, and it is one of Medicare's most valuable benefits. Unlike most Medicare services, qualifying home health care has zero cost to the patient — no copay, no coinsurance, no deductible. But qualifying requires meeting specific eligibility criteria that many patients and families don't fully understand.

The Four Eligibility Requirements

To qualify for Medicare home health services, a patient must meet all four of the following criteria:

1. Homebound Status

The patient must be "homebound" — meaning leaving home requires considerable and taxing effort. This doesn't mean the patient can never leave the house. Medicare allows absences for medical appointments, religious services, adult day programs, and occasional trips. But the patient must generally need help to leave home (walker, wheelchair, special transportation) or have a condition that makes leaving inadvisable.

Homebound status is one of the most commonly misunderstood criteria. A patient who drives to the grocery store twice a week may not meet the standard. A patient who can walk to the mailbox but needs a walker and oxygen to do it likely does.

2. Skilled Care Need

The patient must need at least one of the following skilled services: skilled nursing care on an intermittent basis, physical therapy, speech-language pathology, or continued occupational therapy (OT can sustain eligibility once established by another qualifying service).

The need must be for skilled care — meaning care that requires the training and judgment of a licensed professional. Tasks that can be safely performed by a non-professional (like companionship or light housekeeping) do not qualify.

3. Physician's Order

A physician or allowed practitioner (nurse practitioner, physician assistant, or clinical nurse specialist) must order the home health services and certify that the patient meets the eligibility requirements. The physician must also establish a plan of care that covers all services to be provided.

4. Medicare-Certified Agency

Services must be provided by a home health agency that is certified by Medicare. Not all home care agencies are Medicare-certified — private-duty agencies that provide custodial care only are typically not certified. Verify certification before starting services.

Common misconception: Many families believe that needing help with daily activities (bathing, dressing, cooking) qualifies for Medicare home health. It does not — by itself. Custodial care is covered only when it accompanies skilled care services. If the patient needs a home health aide for personal care but doesn't need skilled nursing or therapy, Medicare will not cover it.

What Services Are Covered

Once eligible, Medicare covers a comprehensive range of home health services at no cost to the patient:

  • Skilled nursing — wound care, injections, medication management, chronic disease education, catheter care, IV therapy, pain management, and assessment of changing conditions
  • Physical therapy — strengthening, balance training, gait training, fall prevention, post-surgical rehabilitation
  • Occupational therapy — activities of daily living training, adaptive equipment, home safety assessment, fine motor rehabilitation
  • Speech-language pathology — swallowing therapy, cognitive-communication rehabilitation, aphasia treatment
  • Medical social services — counseling, care coordination, community resource referrals, advance care planning support
  • Home health aide services — bathing, dressing, grooming assistance, but only when skilled services are also being provided
  • Medical supplies — wound care supplies, ostomy supplies, catheters used during home health visits

What Medicare Does NOT Cover

  • 24-hour home care — Medicare covers intermittent skilled visits, not round-the-clock care
  • Custodial care alone — Help with bathing, meals, and housekeeping without an accompanying skilled need
  • Homemaker services — Cleaning, laundry, shopping, and meal preparation
  • Personal care without skilled services — If skilled nursing or therapy is discontinued, home health aide coverage also stops

How to Start Home Health Services

  1. Talk to the doctor — The patient's physician must agree that home health is medically necessary and order the services
  2. Choose a Medicare-certified agency — The physician may refer to a specific agency, but patients have the right to choose any certified agency in their area
  3. Initial assessment — The agency sends a nurse to assess the patient, complete the OASIS assessment, and develop the plan of care
  4. Physician certification — The physician reviews and signs the plan of care, certifying that the patient meets all eligibility criteria
  5. Services begin — The agency begins providing visits according to the plan of care

How Long Coverage Lasts

Medicare home health has no fixed time limit. Services are authorized in 60-day certification periods (two 30-day episodes). At the end of each period, the physician can recertify the patient for another 60 days if they still meet all eligibility criteria. Patients who have ongoing skilled needs — such as wound care for a chronic non-healing wound or physical therapy after a stroke — can receive home health services for months or years.

Coverage ends when the patient no longer meets eligibility criteria — they're no longer homebound, they no longer need skilled care, or they've met their therapy goals.

For Providers: Certification and Documentation

Home health agencies must maintain meticulous documentation to support Medicare coverage. The three critical documentation elements are the face-to-face encounter (physician must document a face-to-face visit within required timeframes), the OASIS assessment (comprehensive patient assessment that determines the PDGM payment category), and the plan of care (physician-signed document listing all ordered services, visit frequencies, and goals).

Failure in any of these documentation areas can result in claim denial, payment recoupment, or survey deficiencies that jeopardize the agency's Medicare certification.

The Bottom Line

Medicare home health is an exceptional benefit — comprehensive skilled care delivered at home with zero patient cost. The key for patients is meeting the four eligibility criteria, particularly homebound status and the need for skilled care. The key for providers is rigorous documentation of eligibility, accurate OASIS assessment, and timely physician certification. When done right, home health keeps patients safely at home, reduces hospital readmissions, and provides a fully covered benefit that many Medicare beneficiaries don't know they qualify for.
