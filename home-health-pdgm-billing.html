Home Health PDGM Billing: How the Payment Model Works

By Home Health Billing Clarity · April 2026 · 9 min read

The Patient-Driven Groupings Model (PDGM) has been Medicare's payment system for home health care since January 2020, replacing the previous Home Health Prospective Payment System (HHPPS). PDGM fundamentally changed how home health agencies are paid — shifting from a therapy-volume-driven model to a patient-characteristics-driven model. Understanding PDGM is essential for every home health agency's financial sustainability.

How PDGM Calculates Payment

PDGM assigns each 30-day period of care to one of 432 payment categories based on four variables:

1. Admission Source (2 categories)

  • Institutional — Patient was admitted from an acute care hospital, skilled nursing facility, or other institutional setting within 14 days before the home health start of care. These episodes pay more because patients tend to have higher acuity.
  • Community — Patient was referred from the community (physician office, outpatient setting) or was admitted from an institution more than 14 days before the start of care.

2. Timing (2 categories)

  • Early — The first or second 30-day period in a sequence of adjacent episodes. Early episodes pay more because patients typically need more intensive services when first starting home health.
  • Late — The third or subsequent 30-day period. Late episodes pay less, reflecting the expectation that care needs typically decrease over time.

3. Clinical Grouping (12 categories)

The patient's primary diagnosis determines the clinical group. The 12 groups are musculoskeletal rehabilitation, neuro/stroke rehabilitation, wounds, complex nursing interventions, medication management/teaching/assessment (MMTA) — surgical aftercare, MMTA — cardiac/endocrine, MMTA — infectious disease, MMTA — other, behavioral health, and three additional categories. The principal diagnosis on the claim drives the clinical grouping assignment.

4. Functional Impairment Level (3 categories)

Based on OASIS GG items (self-care and mobility scores), patients are classified as low, medium, or high functional impairment. Higher impairment levels receive higher payment. This is where accurate OASIS assessment directly affects revenue.

The math: 2 admission sources × 2 timing categories × 12 clinical groups × 3 functional levels × 3 comorbidity adjustments = 432 possible payment categories. Each category has a different case-mix weight that determines the episode payment when multiplied by the national standardized payment amount.

Comorbidity Adjustments

PDGM also adjusts payment based on secondary diagnoses reported on the claim. Episodes are classified as no comorbidity adjustment, low comorbidity adjustment, or high comorbidity adjustment — based on the interaction between the primary diagnosis clinical group and the secondary diagnoses reported. Accurate and complete secondary diagnosis coding directly affects payment.

Low Utilization Payment Adjustments (LUPA)

If an episode has fewer than a specified number of visits (the LUPA threshold varies by clinical group, typically 2-6 visits), the episode is paid on a per-visit basis rather than the full 30-day bundled rate. LUPA payments are significantly lower than full episode payments. Agencies should monitor LUPA rates — a high LUPA rate indicates potential issues with visit planning, patient attrition, or premature discharge.

Outlier Payments

Episodes with unusually high costs may qualify for outlier payments — additional reimbursement above the standard PDGM rate. To qualify, the estimated cost of the episode must exceed a threshold (the PDGM payment plus a fixed dollar loss amount). Outlier payments partially compensate for the additional cost, not reimburse it fully.

Revenue Optimization Under PDGM

Agencies that maximize revenue under PDGM focus on several areas. Accurate primary diagnosis coding ensures the correct clinical group assignment. Complete secondary diagnosis coding captures all applicable comorbidity adjustments. Precise OASIS functional scoring ensures the functional impairment level reflects the patient's actual status. Managing LUPA risk by planning visit utilization to meet LUPA thresholds ensures full episode payment. Timely admission from institutional sources within the 14-day window captures the higher institutional admission rate.

Common PDGM Billing Errors

  • Primary diagnosis doesn't match clinical picture — The principal diagnosis must reflect the primary reason for home health, which affects clinical grouping and payment
  • Missing comorbidity diagnoses — Failing to report relevant secondary diagnoses leaves money on the table through missed comorbidity adjustments
  • OASIS functional under-scoring — Conservative GG item scoring reduces the functional impairment level and payment
  • LUPA episodes from poor visit planning — Not scheduling enough visits to meet the LUPA threshold converts a full episode payment to a much lower per-visit payment
  • Incorrect admission source determination — Misclassifying an institutional admission as community (or vice versa) changes the payment category

The Bottom Line

PDGM rewards accuracy — in diagnosis coding, OASIS assessment, and visit planning. Agencies that understand the four payment variables and optimize each one through accurate clinical documentation operate with stronger margins than those that treat billing as an afterthought. The shift from therapy-volume to patient-characteristics means that your revenue is determined before the first visit by how well you capture the patient's clinical complexity and functional status.
