What Is Home Health Care? Services, Eligibility, and How It Works

By Home Health Billing Clarity · April 2026 · 8 min read

Home health care is skilled medical care delivered in your home by licensed healthcare professionals — nurses, physical therapists, occupational therapists, speech therapists, and medical social workers. It is prescribed by a physician and provided by Medicare-certified home health agencies for patients who are homebound and need ongoing medical treatment.

Home health care is not the same as home care, personal care, or hospice — though these terms are often confused. Understanding the differences is critical for patients seeking coverage and providers managing compliance.

Home Health Care vs. Home Care vs. Hospice

Home Health Care (Skilled Care)

Medical services provided by licensed professionals — skilled nursing, physical therapy, occupational therapy, speech therapy. Ordered by a physician, covered by Medicare with no patient cost-sharing. Focused on treating a medical condition, rehabilitating after an illness or injury, or managing a chronic disease. Provided on an intermittent basis (scheduled visits, not 24-hour care).

Home Care (Custodial/Personal Care)

Non-medical assistance with activities of daily living — bathing, dressing, grooming, meal preparation, light housekeeping, companionship. Provided by home health aides or personal care assistants who are not necessarily licensed clinicians. Generally NOT covered by Medicare unless provided alongside qualifying skilled services. Often paid privately or through Medicaid long-term care programs.

Hospice Care

Comfort-focused care for patients with a terminal illness and a life expectancy of six months or less. Covers medical care, pain management, emotional support, and spiritual care. Covered by Medicare Part A under a separate hospice benefit. Patients elect hospice by choosing comfort care over curative treatment.

What Services Does Home Health Include?

A home health care episode can include any combination of skilled nursing visits for wound care, medication management, disease education, IV therapy, and patient monitoring. Physical therapy for mobility, strength, balance, and fall prevention. Occupational therapy for daily living skills, home safety, and adaptive techniques. Speech-language pathology for swallowing disorders, communication rehabilitation, and cognitive therapy. Medical social services for care coordination, counseling, and community resource navigation. Home health aide services for personal care when skilled services are also active.

Who Qualifies?

For Medicare-covered home health, patients must be homebound (leaving home is a considerable effort), need skilled nursing or therapy services, be under a physician's care with an active plan of treatment, and receive services from a Medicare-certified home health agency. For a detailed eligibility guide, see our article on Medicare home health coverage.

How Home Health Visits Work

A typical home health episode begins with a comprehensive assessment by a registered nurse. During this first visit, the nurse evaluates the patient's medical condition, reviews medications, assesses the home environment for safety, and completes the OASIS assessment — a standardized data collection tool required by CMS. Based on this assessment, the nurse and the patient's physician develop a plan of care that specifies which services will be provided, how often, and what goals the treatment aims to achieve.

Subsequent visits follow the plan of care. A patient recovering from hip replacement surgery might receive skilled nursing visits twice weekly plus physical therapy three times weekly for the first month, then taper to once weekly as they regain mobility. Each visit is documented, and progress toward goals is tracked and reported to the physician.

How Many Hours Per Week?

Home health care is visit-based, not hour-based. A typical skilled nursing visit lasts 30-60 minutes. A therapy visit lasts 45-60 minutes. The number of visits per week varies by patient need — some patients receive daily visits immediately after hospital discharge, while others need only one or two visits per week for chronic disease management.

Medicare does not cover 8-hour or 24-hour home care shifts. If a patient needs around-the-clock care, that falls under custodial care and is typically paid privately or through Medicaid waiver programs.

For families: If your loved one needs both skilled medical care and ongoing daily assistance (help with bathing, meals, supervision), you may need two separate services — Medicare-covered home health for the skilled component, and private-pay or Medicaid home care for the custodial component. A hospital discharge planner or the home health agency's intake coordinator can help navigate this.

How Providers Deliver Home Health Care

Home health agencies manage a team of clinicians who travel to patients' homes throughout the day. The agency is responsible for staffing and scheduling all visits, maintaining compliance with Medicare Conditions of Participation, completing OASIS assessments accurately and on time, securing physician orders and plan of care signatures, billing Medicare under the PDGM payment model, and tracking patient outcomes and quality measures reported to CMS.

Quality performance directly affects the agency's reimbursement through the Home Health Value-Based Purchasing (HHVBP) program, which adjusts Medicare payments up or down based on quality scores.

The Bottom Line

Home health care is skilled medical treatment delivered where patients are most comfortable — at home. It covers a wide range of clinical services, is fully paid by Medicare for qualifying patients, and serves as a critical bridge between hospital care and independent living. For patients and families, the most important step is understanding whether you meet the eligibility criteria. For providers, the most important step is documenting that eligibility accurately at every stage of the care episode.
