How Much Does Medicare Pay for Home Health Care Per Hour?

By Home Health Billing Clarity · April 2026 · 9 min read

This is one of the most searched questions about Medicare home health — and the answer surprises most people. Medicare does not pay for home health care by the hour. Instead, Medicare pays home health agencies a lump sum per 30-day episode of care under the Patient-Driven Groupings Model (PDGM). Understanding how this payment model actually works is essential for both providers managing their margins and families trying to understand what Medicare will cover.

How Medicare Home Health Payment Works

Since January 2020, Medicare has used the Patient-Driven Groupings Model (PDGM) to pay for home health services. Under PDGM, Medicare pays the home health agency a single bundled payment for each 30-day period of care. This payment covers all skilled services provided during that period — nursing visits, therapy visits, home health aide services, medical social services, and medical supplies.

The agency receives one payment regardless of how many visits are provided during the 30-day period. If the agency provides 12 visits, it gets the same base payment as if it provides 8 visits. This creates a financial incentive for agencies to deliver efficient, outcomes-focused care.

What Determines the Payment Amount

PDGM calculates the per-episode payment based on four factors:

  • Admission source — Whether the patient was referred from a hospital/post-acute facility (institutional) or from the community. Institutional admissions pay more because patients tend to be sicker.
  • Timing — Whether this is an early episode (first or second 30-day period) or a late episode (third or later). Early episodes pay more.
  • Clinical grouping — The patient's primary diagnosis determines which of 12 clinical groups they fall into (e.g., musculoskeletal, neuro/stroke, wounds, cardiac).
  • Functional impairment level — Based on OASIS assessment scores, patients are classified into functional levels that reflect their care needs.

These four factors combine to place each episode into one of 432 possible payment categories, each with a different payment amount.

Approximate Payment Ranges

While the exact payment depends on the PDGM category, approximate ranges for 2026 are:

  • Low-acuity community episodes: ,500–,200 per 30 days
  • Medium-acuity episodes: ,200–,500 per 30 days
  • High-acuity institutional episodes: ,500–,500+ per 30 days

Why the "per hour" question is misleading: When families search "how much does Medicare pay per hour," they're usually trying to understand whether they can get a home health aide for 8 hours a day paid by Medicare. The answer is generally no — Medicare home health covers skilled intermittent care (visits), not full-time custodial care. Full-time home care is typically a Medicaid or private-pay service.

What Medicare Home Health Covers

Medicare home health covers skilled services provided on an intermittent basis:

  • Skilled nursing — wound care, medication management, IV therapy, patient education, disease management
  • Physical therapy — rehabilitation, mobility training, fall prevention
  • Occupational therapy — activities of daily living, adaptive equipment training
  • Speech-language pathology — swallowing therapy, communication rehabilitation
  • Medical social services — care coordination, community resource connection
  • Home health aide services — personal care assistance, but only when skilled services are also being provided

The critical limitation is "intermittent" — Medicare does not cover 24-hour home care, live-in caregivers, or custodial care (help with bathing, dressing, meals) unless it's provided alongside skilled nursing or therapy services.

What Patients Pay

For patients who meet Medicare's eligibility requirements, home health care has no copay, no coinsurance, and no deductible. It is one of the few Medicare benefits with zero patient cost-sharing. Medicare pays the agency directly, and the patient owes nothing — as long as the services are medically necessary and provided by a Medicare-certified home health agency.

The only exception is durable medical equipment (DME) ordered through home health, which follows Medicare's standard 80/20 cost-sharing after the Part B deductible.

How Long Medicare Pays

There is no specific time limit on Medicare home health services. As long as the patient continues to meet eligibility criteria — homebound status, need for skilled care, under a physician's plan of care — Medicare will continue to authorize 30-day episodes. Patients can receive home health services for months or even years if medically necessary.

However, each 30-day episode requires recertification by the ordering physician, and the home health agency must demonstrate continued need through OASIS assessments and progress documentation.

For Providers: Managing Margins Under PDGM

Because PDGM pays a fixed amount per episode regardless of visit count, agency profitability depends on delivering the right number of visits — enough to achieve good outcomes, but not so many that costs exceed the episode payment. Key strategies include accurate OASIS coding to ensure proper clinical grouping and functional scoring, front-loading visits early in the episode when patients typically need the most care, monitoring visit utilization against the episode payment to identify margin pressure, and reducing unnecessary therapy visits that were historically volume-driven under the old payment model.

The Bottom Line

Medicare doesn't pay for home health care by the hour — it pays agencies a bundled rate per 30-day episode under PDGM. For patients, the good news is that qualifying home health care is fully covered with no out-of-pocket cost. For providers, understanding how PDGM calculates payment is essential for sustainable operations. The agencies that thrive under PDGM are the ones that code accurately, manage visit utilization efficiently, and deliver outcomes that keep patients from being rehospitalized.
